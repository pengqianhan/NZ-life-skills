# JP Skill Current Guidance

As of: `2026-05-28`

This reference summarizes stable baseline guidance for New Zealand Justice of the Peace (JP, 太平绅士) questions. JP service scope and local desk details can change, so verify official sources before giving operational details such as addresses, appointment rules, opening hours, or acceptance boundaries.

## Stable Baseline

New Zealand JPs can perform common ministerial functions such as witnessing documents, taking declarations, and swearing affidavits or affirming affirmations. The Ministry of Justice describes these as JP duties, while Govt.nz lists a JP as one kind of official witness for statutory declarations.

Use JP as a practical option when the user needs:

- a certified copy / certified true copy, after the JP sees the original document
- a signature witnessed on a document that accepts JP witnessing
- a statutory declaration signed and witnessed face to face
- an affidavit sworn, or an affirmation made, before the JP
- an ordinary certified-document request where the receiving organisation accepts JP certification

## Certified Copies

For certified copies, the JP must see the original document. The user should bring the original and the copy or printout to be certified. For bank or financial institution use, the person whose document is being certified may need to attend and bring ID.

Explain that certification means the copy matches the original seen by the JP. It does not mean the JP has independently verified that the facts in the document are true.

## Signatures, Declarations, Affidavits, And Affirmations

For witnessed signatures, statutory declarations, affidavits, and affirmations, the user should not sign before seeing the JP. The JP generally needs to personally see the user sign, declare, swear, or affirm in person.

For statutory declarations, Govt.nz says the person must meet an official witness in person and cannot use e-signatures or video calls. The user is responsible for making sure the declaration is correct.

## Cost

JP services are free of charge. If the user needs copying or printing, there may be separate copying costs depending on the place or service desk.

## Notary, Apostille, Authentication, And Overseas Documents

A JP is not the same as a New Zealand notary public. If the receiving organisation asks for "notarised", "apostille", "authentication", or a document for overseas official use, verify the exact requirements.

Govt.nz Authentication Unit guidance says some documents need notarisation by a New Zealand notary public, a government-issued original, or certification by the issuing government department. For New Zealand visa and New Zealand driver licence documents used for Authentication Unit purposes, copies must be notarised by an NZ notary public. Govt.nz also warns not to use JP or solicitor certification when a notary is required for apostille or authentication.

## Visa-Specific Triage

When a user asks about certifying a visa:

- If the receiving organisation only wants a certified copy of an eVisa, visa label, or approval letter and accepts JP certification, a JP may be enough.
- If the receiving organisation wants confirmation that the visa is currently valid, direct them to Immigration New Zealand verification channels rather than JP certification.
- If the receiving organisation wants notarisation, apostille, authentication, or overseas official use, direct the user to the appropriate notary or Authentication Unit process.

## Finding A JP

After confirming JP is appropriate, search for local options using:

- Royal Federation Find a JP directory
- regional JP association service desk pages
- District Court, council, library, university, or CAB pages that host JP service desks

When presenting options, include:

- source link
- address or area
- contact method or booking instructions
- service desk hours if available
- whether appointment is required
- any notes about bringing original documents, copies, ID, or unsigned forms
